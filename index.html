Understanding Video Copyright in the US copyright

Video copyright law offers protection to creators of “original works of authorship”. When you wish to use a video or audio clip that is protected by copyright, you must seek permission from the copyright holder.

However, there are many ways to source content that is not copyrightable, or in the public domain.

You can also re-use video content under copyright, providing you do so in line with “fair use” best practice

What is Fair Use ?

Fair use is a flexible set of rules and expectations that allows the re-use of video and audio content without permission or payment. There are the four things we must consider:

What are we doing with the content ?

If we transform the content into something new we are likely to be covered under fair use.

What is the nature of the content ?

Non-fiction material such as live event coverage or documentary footage is more likely to be okay to use than fictional, creative works.

How much of the content are we using ?

We can only use snippets of copyrighted content to demonstrate what we want to say.

Will we be offering content that’s similar to the original ?

We can only use snippets of copyrighted content that will not take potential sales away from the original.

Examples of When it’s Okay to Use Copyrighted Video and Audio

Fair Use of Movie, TV and Spoken Word Clips

It is considered fair use to quote copyrighted movie, TV or audio clips providing you are using it to illustrate an argument or point.

If one of our videos is demonstrating specific locations used to film iconic movies, we can use clips from those movies under fair use. Similarly, if we want to demonstrate a particular celebrity’s opinion on a topic, we can use a clip of them speaking on that topic in public.

We can also use copyrighted video and audio to create a mashup. A mashup is a creative mix-up of content that can serve a number of purposes, such as a tribute to the original or parody of it.

Copyrighted Material Accidentally Captured on Camera

When Valoso is filming on site, it’s always possible that we’ll accidentally capture copyrighted material on camera. For example, we may capture:

  • A TV in the background of one of our shots playing a copyrighted film.
  • A radio or live band playing a popular copyrighted song.
  • An original work of copyrighted art hanging on the wall.

None of these examples are considered an infringement on video copyright.

Privacy and Live Events

format_quote

The law on recording video and audio footage of people is complicated and in the US it can differ from state to state.

A general rule of thumb is that it’s legal to record someone in public providing they don’t have what’s known as “an expectation of privacy”.

This covers places with washrooms and changing rooms but not, say, a live event that’s open to the public, the mall or the subway.

Private business can ask you to stop recording video on their premises, but until they ask you to do so, you are free to record.

These rules can also differ country-by-country. At Valoso we take privacy and video copyright laws very seriously and are always respectful of local customs and expectations when shooting.

Understandin Creative Commons and Video Copyright

With content, we don’t need to worry about fair use at all. That’s because the work is licensed for public use by Creative Commons. Creative Commons is a non-profit organization makes it easier for people to copyright their work. There are three types of Creative Commons license.

All rights
reserved

An “all rights reserved” license means the copyrighter wishes to retain all of their rights and we can only use their content if it’s considered fair use (as above).

Some rights
reserved

A “some rights reserved” license means copyright holders are comfortable with their work being re-used in some ways but not others.

Public domain

And a “public domain” license means anyone can copy, distribute, display or modify the work with proper attribution.

Great Sources for Video and Audio in the Public Domain

You can find great free-to-use content online in a number of places.

shareVideo sharing platforms

Video sharing platforms like Youtube and Vimeo includes a selection of videos licensed by Creative Commons that can be re-used, modified and even used commercially.

archiveThe Moving Image Archive

The Moving Image Archive is the film and video section of the Internet Archive, a global non-profit that exists to preserve our civilization’s digital cultural artifacts.

image_searchThe Moving Image Research Center

The Moving Image Research Center is a division of the Library of Congress and holds a collection of early motion pictures and television, all of which are free of video copyright and in the public domain.

The Memphis Belle: A Story of a Flying Fortress courtesy of Library of Congress, Motion Picture, Broadcasting, and Recorded Sound Division

perm_mediaWikimedia Commons

Wikimedia Commons is a global repository of media files sourced by the public. Each file is either fully in the public domain or licensed under Creative Commons.

audiotrackPublic domain audio clips

Public domain audio clips include historical music and audio files with an educational use, such as those offered by NASA. You can find contemporary music and audio sources licensed by creative commons via a few sites including ccMixter, Freesound and Opsound.

This is not an exhaustive list and there are more sources of public domain content to discover.
